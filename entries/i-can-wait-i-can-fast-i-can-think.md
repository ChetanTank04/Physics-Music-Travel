<p>The statement is from &lsquo;Siddhartha&rsquo;, the classic novel by Hermann Hesse, a German Nobel laureate. I have read it in three languages &mdash; Gujarati, Hindi and English &mdash; at different ages. In Gujarati I read it when I was 13, in Hindi around 19 or 20, in English I read it recently at 38. Originally it is written in German. I would love to read it in German, obviously after learning it. It&rsquo;s one of the all-time greats for me.</p>
<p>When I was in Lokbharti, Sanosara, Prof. Ayyangar, who was vice chancellor of Gujarat Vidhyapith at that time, visited Lokbharti for a function. In his speech he said <em>&ldquo;jis ka nind or bhukh par kabu nahi hai, woh kabhi rishi nahi ban sakta.&rdquo;</em> Let&rsquo;s say the ability to fast is at the core of many religious processes and even holds such a high place in Jainism. It&rsquo;s all about having control over yourself, your wishes and pleasures. It is about establishing authority over oneself.</p>
<p>I think fasting is a way to get control over one&rsquo;s internal being, and patience gives you control over external factors. How much you can wait for anything is at the core of winning or getting something done. It&rsquo;s not about doing nothing and just waiting; it is about not being defeated at any cost and keeping on working for the decided aim. Patience gives you power over everyone.</p>
<p>A person who can wait can bring bigger changes in the world, and a person who can fast can change one&rsquo;s self. A person who can think can observe, evaluate and evolve himself &mdash; can think about the creator and creation, thinking what makes us human. And if one person can do all three&hellip;</p>
